# Conclusions and Abstract

Synthesize only stable manuscript evidence. Write conclusions first; write the abstract last.

## Write conclusions

For each conclusion, record its supporting result, scope, uncertainty, and limiting method or assumption. Then write:

1. direct answer to the research question;
2. principal findings with no new results;
3. bounded interpretation and contribution;
4. limitations tied to actual design, methods, data, assumptions, or execution;
5. future work tied to a named limitation, unresolved result, or justified next test.

Remove claims that lack a result trace. Do not broaden from one population, setting, metric, or model beyond supported scope. Do not add citations or evidence that were not established in the manuscript.

## Write the abstract last

Start only after results, discussion, and conclusions are stable. Follow the required structure and word limit when provided. Otherwise include compact context, objective, design/method, principal results with verified quantitative detail where available, and a calibrated conclusion.

Every abstract statement must resolve to the manuscript. Keep methods and results proportional; avoid background padding, undefined abbreviations, unsupported impact, and claims stronger than the conclusion.

If stable results are unavailable, do not draft a results-bearing abstract or invent placeholders that look factual. Provide a labeled structure, list the exact missing fields, and pause synthesis. Missing evidence blocks the corresponding conclusion or narrows it.
